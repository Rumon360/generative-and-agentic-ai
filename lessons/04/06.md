## Vector Embeddings

This is the stage where your input to an LLM is first tokenized into numerical values and then these numbers are converted into something known as vector embeddings.

---

For example, suppose I say to you, “The dog ate the cat.” When you read this sentence, you naturally imagine a dog eating a cat.

Similarly, if I simply say “the mobile,” you automatically picture a mobile phone—perhaps even the one you are using right now.

If I mention “Paris,” you might instantly imagine the Eiffel Tower, charming streets, or a romantic European city.

Now, as you read these lines, something interesting is happening in your brain. When I mention the word “dog,” you begin to imagine a dog. When I refer to a “cat,” you picture a cat, and when I say “Paris,” you form a mental image of that city—and so on.

At the end of the day, all of these are just English characters jumbled alphabets on a page. Yet somehow, they carry meaning that you can instantly understand and visualize.

So how can we get a machine to understand these meanings in a similar way? This is where vector embeddings come in.

---

Vector embeddings essentially give semantic meaning to tokens.

Semantic meaning is the meaning or idea that a word or phrase represents.

---

### How Vector Embeddings Work

1. **Break Text into Tokens:**
   The input sentence is split into smaller units called tokens. These can be full words or parts of words, depending on the tokenizer.

   - Example: _“The dog ate the cat”_ → `[The, dog, ate, the, cat]`

   In some models, it could even be:

   - `[The, dog, at, e, the, cat]`

---

2. **Map Tokens to IDs (Numbers):**
   Each token is mapped to a unique number using a predefined vocabulary.

   - Example:

     - `The → 101`
     - `dog → 205`
     - `cat → 309`

   At this stage, these numbers **do not carry meaning**—they are just identifiers.

---

3. **Convert Token IDs into Vectors (Embeddings):**
   Each token ID is passed through an **embedding layer**, which converts it into a vector (a list of numbers).

   - Example:

     - `dog → [0.21, -0.44, 0.90, ...]`
     - `cat → [0.19, -0.40, 0.85, ...]`

   These vectors typically have hundreds or even thousands of dimensions.

   👉 You can think of this as a lookup table where each token ID retrieves its corresponding vector.

---

4. **How These Vectors Get Meaning:**
   Initially, these vectors are random. During training, the model adjusts them based on context.

   For example:

   - The model often sees “dog” and “cat” used in similar contexts.
   - It learns to make their vectors similar.

   Over time:

   - Similar words → similar vectors
   - Different words → different vectors

   This is how **semantic meaning is learned automatically**.

---

5. **Position Matters (Positional Encoding):**
   Since models process tokens in parallel, they also need to understand order.

   So, additional information is added to each vector to represent its position in the sentence.

   - Example:

     - “dog bites man” ≠ “man bites dog”

   Without position, both sentences would look the same to the model.

---

6. **Contextual Understanding (Dynamic Embeddings):**
   In modern LLMs, embeddings are not fixed—they change depending on context.

   - Example:

     - “bank” in _“river bank”_
     - “bank” in _“money bank”_

   The model produces different vectors for the same word based on surrounding words.

---

7. **Measuring Similarity (Vector Distance):**
   Once words are converted into vectors, the model can compare them using distance or similarity metrics.

   Common idea:

   - Closer vectors → more similar meaning
   - Farther vectors → less related

   Example relationships:

   - “dog” ↔ “cat” → close
   - “Paris” ↔ “London” → close
   - “dog” ↔ “Paris” → far

---

8. **Why This Matters:**
   Because everything is now numbers, the model can:

   - Find similar words
   - Understand relationships
   - Perform reasoning
   - Generate meaningful text

   In short, **vector embeddings turn language into something a machine can understand and work with mathematically**.
