## How LLMs Work

When I ask an LLM something, for example, "Hey," I get a response. So what’s basically happening is:

1. The user inputs something — in our case, "Hey" — into the LLM.
2. The LLM responds with something like, "Hey there, how are you?"

That’s what the LLM is doing.

The important question is: **how does this "Hey" get understood by the LLM, and how does it magically produce a meaningful response?**

---

### Terminology

1. **Input Tokens** – The text the user inputs (in our case: "Hey").
2. **Output Tokens** – The text generated by the LLM (in our case: "Hey there, how are you?").

We are essentially chatting with the "GPT." The inner workings of the LLM are hidden behind this name. But what does it really mean?

**GPT** stands for **Generative Pre-Trained Transformer**. Let’s break it down.

1. **Generative** – This means the model can **generate new content**. Whenever you give it an input, it creates a response on the spot. Unlike Google, which only retrieves existing relevant links, an LLM doesn’t search for answers — it **generates them**.

2. **Pre-Trained** – LLMs generate responses based on **pre-trained data**. They don’t just output random text; they rely on patterns learned from massive datasets during training. This pre-training allows them to produce coherent, relevant responses.

3. **Transformer** – LLM is acutally a transformer which is generative in nature and generative on the basis of pre-trained data.

So, every LLM you interact with is essentially a **transformer model that generates text based on pre-trained data**. That’s why all these LLMs fall under the umbrella of "GPT."
