## Understanding Multi-Head Attention for Rich Context

Before jumping into this, let me introduce another important concept called **Self-Attention**.

This is the step where the vector embeddings start “talking” to each other. In other words, each word looks at other words in the sentence and updates its meaning based on context.

---

### What is Self-Attention?

Let’s take an example:

- _“River bank”_
- _“Brac Bank”_

If you look at the position of the word _“bank”_, it is the same (second position) in both phrases. However, the meanings are completely different.

- In _“river bank”_ → it means the side of a river
- In _“Brac Bank”_ → it refers to a financial institution

So, even though the position is the same, the meaning changes based on context.

---

### How Self-Attention Solves This

Self-attention allows each word’s vector embedding to interact with other word embeddings in the sentence.

- The _“bank”_ embedding looks at surrounding words
- If it interacts with _“river”_, its meaning shifts toward nature
- If it interacts with _“Brac”_, its meaning shifts toward finance

So essentially:

> The meaning of a word is updated based on the words around it.

This is the core idea of **self-attention**.

---

## What is Multi-Head Attention?

Now, instead of doing this process just once, the model does it **multiple times in parallel**, focusing on different aspects each time.

This is called **Multi-Head Attention**.

---

### Intuition Behind Multi-Head Attention

Let’s use your example:

There is a train passing by, and you are observing it from a distance. In one of the compartments, you notice a dog sleeping.

Now, different parts of your brain focus on different details:

- One part thinks: _“That’s a very cute dog.”_
- Another part thinks: _“It looks like a Labrador.”_
- Another part notices: _“It is sleeping near the door of a moving train.”_

So, at the same time, you are analyzing:

- appearance
- type
- position
- situation

---

### Connecting This to Multi-Head Attention

Each of these “brain cells” is like an **attention head**.

- One head focuses on relationships (who relates to whom)
- One head focuses on meaning
- One head focuses on position or structure
- Another head may focus on specific details

All of these heads work **in parallel**, and their outputs are combined.

---

### Why Multi-Head Attention is Powerful

- It captures **multiple perspectives** of the same sentence
- It improves **context understanding**
- It helps resolve ambiguity (like “bank”)
- It allows richer and deeper language understanding
